About the project

MOMO is a startup that is developing an AI-powered coaching app focused on self-reflection, personal development, and mental well-being — including features connected to the menstrual cycle.

As part of our education at STI, our group was given the opportunity to collaborate with MOMO as a real client. The assignment was to evaluate and improve their app through user testing, analysis, and concrete design suggestions.

Method

We carried out a qualitative usability test with participants who belong to MOMO's target group and had not previously tested the app. The "think aloud" method was used throughout — the participants described what they were thinking while they used the app.

Target group

Participants who fit MOMO's user profile and had no prior experience of the app

Execution

We conducted quick, qualitative testing to gather insights on how users perceived the new design.

  • Methodology: Guerrilla testing to gather quick, qualitative feedback. The sessions were conducted by a two-person team — one facilitator guiding the test and one observer taking notes.
  • Session Structure:
    1. First Impressions — Evaluating initial reactions to the new homepage, focusing on what users perceived the site to offer.
    2. Design Comparison — A side-by-side comparison of the new versus the old design, based on the parameters safe and intuitive.
  • Participants: 5 female participants, spontaneously recruited on-site at the school campus.

Our iteration suggestions

Based on the insights, the group developed the following improvement proposals, prioritized by impact and effort:

High priority

  • Move cycle info from chat view to Insights
  • Show that Insights contains graphs/charts more clearly
  • Rename "Conversation themes" → "Recently emerging themes"
  • Clarify the difference between the two insight categories
  • Add a 4th chat mode: Feedback — that summarizes trends using collected data

For later

  • Link cycle phase to chat when relevant
  • Connect cycle patterns to mood patterns and themes
  • Research what users actually need in the Insights view
  • Add dynamic elements: typing animations, animated Insights, responsive UI states

Final overview of the project outcomes and priorities.

My learnings

1. Working with a real client is different

When there's a real startup behind the product, the feedback feels more significant. I noticed I was listening more carefully and thinking about how my observations could actually help the team build something better.

2. Words matter just as much as design

Several of the problems users encountered were about labels and wording not the visual design itself. This reinforced how important it is to involve users early, before everything is "finished."

3. Users don't read they scan

Even when information was right in front of them, participants missed it. Hierarchy, positioning, and visual cues are crucial to guide the user's eye.

4. My background gives me an advantage

Years of working in customer service and care have trained me to listen actively and read between the lines. During the tests, I could pick up on hesitations and emotions that might have gone unnoticed and that's where the real insights hide.
